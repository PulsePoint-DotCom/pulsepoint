Who Is Puckwudgie?

Hey — I'm Puckwudgie, and I'm an 8th grader in New York. I built PulsePoint because I was tired of opening five different apps just to figure out what was actually going on in the world. Tech news is here, sports is there, some random TikTok trend is somewhere else entirely.

PulsePoint started as a way to solve my own problem: I wanted one place that had the good stuff, written in a way that doesn't talk down to you. I'm into coding, robotics, XC track, snowboarding, and building things — so those topics tend to show up a lot. But I cover everything that seems worth your time.

This site is run by me, with help from a small team of editors who specialize in different areas — tech, sports, culture, music, and viral internet stuff. Every article goes through editing before it goes up.

"I wanted PulsePoint to feel like a smart friend who stays up to date on everything and tells you the interesting parts — without making you feel like you're reading a textbook." — Puckwudgie, on why PulsePoint exists

Editorial Standards

Every article on PulsePoint is researched and written to be accurate, readable, and genuinely useful. I don't just summarize press releases — I try to add context, real examples, and my own take on why something matters.

If I get something wrong, I want to know. Use the contact page to flag errors or suggest topics. I'm not perfect, but I try to be honest and careful.

All opinions in articles are clearly labeled as analysis, and news reporting is separated from commentary. Sources are cited where relevant.

Beyond PulsePoint

When I'm not writing articles or obsessing over what's trending, I'm probably training for cross-country, coding robots, or helping out with my robotics team. I've been coding for a few years now — Python, some JavaScript, and whatever the robotics competition requires that week.

I got into web development because I wanted to actually build something real on the internet, not just post on social media. PulsePoint is that project. It's a work in progress, and it probably always will be — but that's kind of the point.
