Waymo Now Open to Everyone in Orlando and Miami

April 15, 2026 · 3 min read

🚗

Waymo announced today that its robotaxi service is now open to anyone in Orlando and Miami — no more waitlist, no more invitation. Download the app, and you can hail a fully autonomous ride right now. This is a big deal for the self-driving car industry.

What Happened

Waymo typically rolls out new cities slowly — invite-only groups, then a waitlist, then a broader launch. But this time they're skipping straight to "anyone with the app." The company says riders in Miami can take routes on actual freeways, which could make trips faster than the surface-street-only routes some competitors use.

Orlando is a natural fit — it's a tourist destination with heavy traffic and lots of people who might prefer not to deal with parking. Waymo's been testing in both cities for over a year.

The Bull Case

✓ This proves self-driving is ready: Waymo has logged millions of miles with a strong safety record. Opening to everyone — not just hand-picked testers — shows real confidence. It also gives the company more real-world data to improve. And for riders, no more tips, no more surge pricing drama, just clean autonomous transport.

The Bear Case

✗ Still limited geography: Waymo only operates in a handful of cities. For most Americans, this changes nothing. And while Waymo's safety record is good, any incident with an autonomous vehicle gets outsized attention. One high-profile accident could stall the entire industry. Plus, these cars still can't handle true extreme weather — heavy rain, snow, floods.

The Bigger Picture

Waymo is owned by Google/Alphabet. Its main competitor, Cruise (backed by GM), had a rough 2023 after a pedestrian incident in San Francisco and is still rebuilding trust. Meanwhile, Tesla's "Full Self-Driving" remains controversial — it requires human oversight and has been criticized for being oversold. Waymo's approach of going fully autonomous with no one behind the wheel is different from Tesla's approach, and the results are starting to diverge.

The question isn't whether autonomous vehicles will become normal — it's whether it happens in 5 years or 20, and who gets there first.
